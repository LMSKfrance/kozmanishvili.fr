01 — Overview

A claims platform that had grown too complex to use well.

Symmetra's insurtech platform served over 100,000 users across four product lines, inside a GxP-validated, Solvency II-reported environment. At its core, the claims handling workflow had accreted into an eleven-step linear process — reliable, auditable, but slow, error-prone, and punishing to learn.

As Lead Product Designer I drove end-to-end UX from contextual research through high-fidelity interaction design and a token-first component system — working closely with product owners, engineering leads and compliance stakeholders to ship a solution that scaled across every product line and role. I mentored three junior designers throughout.

Constraints

Enterprise reliability, multi-role access for agents, handlers and admins, a complete audit trail, and data-dense interfaces that couldn't lose information.

Mandate

Cut handling time and errors without sacrificing the rigor — authority limits, validation, traceability — a regulated claims process demands.

02 — The Problem

Eleven sequential steps, each a chance to stall or err.

Handlers moved through eleven discrete screens to settle a single claim. Every step demanded re-entry, re-orientation, and a decision the interface didn't help them make. New hires took weeks to reach competence; experienced handlers still made avoidable errors under load.

03 — Before

The interface claims handlers actually used.

The legacy system had grown one step at a time — each addition justified, each one narrowing the corridor further. By 2020 it was an eleven-screen linear wizard: no live data lookup, no role awareness, no memory of what the handler had entered three steps ago.

Legacy CMS v2.4 — Step 1 of 11. Policy entry deferred to Step 2; coverage locked behind Step 4. No live lookup anywhere in the flow.

A

Data entered on one step was asked for again on another

Policy number was typed on Step 2 but not looked up until Step 3. Coverage details that lived in the policy record still required manual entry on Step 4. Handlers corrected their own input constantly.

B

Related decisions were separated by four screens

Liability (Step 7), reserve (Step 8) and settlement (Step 9) were logically one decision. Splitting them across three screens forced handlers to hold context in their heads — and introduced errors when that context slipped under load.

C

No role awareness — every user walked every step

Agents, handlers and admins shared the same eleven-screen wizard regardless of what they actually needed to do. The system had no concept of role context — it presented every field to every person, every time.

04 — Research

Three rounds of contextual research, iterated into design.

Using Human-Centered Design methods — contextual inquiry, task analysis, and three rounds of usability testing with 12 claims handlers — I shadowed agents during live claim processing to find where real friction lived, not where the org chart assumed it did. Rapid low-fidelity prototypes were tested and discarded at each stage before any high-fidelity work began.

A

Most steps gathered data the system already had

Handlers re-keyed information that existed elsewhere in the record — policy, coverage, claimant — a prime target for smart defaults and live verification.

B

Decisions clustered, but screens didn't

Related judgments — liability, severity, reserve — were scattered across separate steps, forcing constant context-switching mid-claim.

C

Roles needed different views of one truth

Agents, handlers and admins each needed a tailored view — but of the same underlying claim data, not divergent forks of it.

05 — Design Process

Three phases, twelve handlers, three rounds of testing.

The process was evidence-led and iterative. No part of the new flow was committed to high-fidelity until it had been validated at low-fidelity first. The consolidation from eleven steps to three screens wasn't imposed — it followed the decision clusters handlers themselves described when shadowed in context.

01

Discover — Contextual inquiry and journey mapping

I shadowed 12 handlers over two weeks, mapping every screen visit, every back-navigation, every sticky note. The journey map surfaced 11 friction clusters — one per wizard step. The deeper finding: the architecture mirrored the database schema, not how handlers actually thought about settling a claim.

02

Define — HMW framing and rapid lo-fi testing

“How might we let a handler make an intake decision without leaving the intake screen?” Four low-fidelity concepts were sketched and tested in a single day. Three were discarded. The surviving one grouped screens by decision type — not data type — and consistently reduced task time in unmoderated sessions with handlers.

03

Deliver — Mid-fi to token-first to high-fidelity

Mid-fidelity mocks were tested in two further rounds, each tightening the layout and validation model. The design system was built in parallel: tokens defined before components, components before screens. Engineering was brought in only after two usability rounds confirmed the structure held.

07 — Design System

One system, every role, four product lines.

The redesign held together because it sat on a token-first shared component library — designed to be contributed to and extended, not owned by a single project. A five-state semantic palette made every badge, alert and table row accessible by construction. A two-typeface rule separated human judgment from machine-generated data. A single radius unified the whole surface. Every decision was codified so cross-functional teams — product, engineering, QA — could reason about it without the designer in the room.

08 — States & Edge Cases

In a regulated tool, the edges are the product.

In complex, regulated employee-facing tools, interaction design is governance. Every field carries an empty, loading, verified and error state — designed as a complete set, not an afterthought. Authority limits are enforced in the flow itself, not as a post-submit error. The interface never lets a handler act past their permission — it routes them forward through the correct approval path instead.

09 — Across the Workspace

The same library, composed into every surface.

Beyond the core flow, the same component library composed into policy search, financials, document management, role-based claim views and reporting — each data-dense, each accountable, each a new surface built without new decisions. This is what scalable design looks like in a global, multi-role product ecosystem: one system, not eleven parallel ones.

10 — Outcome

Faster, more accurate, easier to onboard.

The redesigned flow cut handling time by over a third while errors and support load fell sharply — and new handlers reached competence faster. The system outlived the project, carrying every product line on one component library.
