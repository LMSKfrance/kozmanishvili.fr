01 — Overview

A claims platform that had grown too complex to use well.

Symmetra's insurtech platform served over 100,000 users across four product lines. At its core, the claims handling workflow had accreted into an eleven-step linear process — reliable, but slow, error-prone, and punishing to learn.

As Lead Product Designer I owned research, information architecture, and the design system that let the redesign scale across every product line — while mentoring three junior designers through the work.

Constraints

Enterprise reliability, multi-role access for agents, handlers and admins, and data-dense interfaces that couldn't lose information.

Mandate

Cut handling time and errors without sacrificing the rigor a regulated claims process demands.

02 — The Problem

Eleven sequential steps, each a chance to stall or err.

Handlers moved through eleven discrete screens to settle a single claim. Every step demanded re-entry, re-orientation, and a decision the interface didn't help them make. New hires took weeks to reach competence; experienced handlers still made avoidable errors under load.

03 — Research

I shadowed handlers processing live claims.

I ran contextual inquiry with 12 claims handlers across three rounds of usability testing, and shadowed agents during live claim processing to see where the real friction lived — not where the org chart assumed it did.

A

Most steps gathered data the system already had

Handlers re-keyed information that existed elsewhere in the record — a prime target for smart defaults.

B

Decisions clustered, but screens didn't

Related judgments were scattered across separate steps, forcing constant context-switching mid-claim.

C

Roles needed different views of one truth

Agents, handlers and admins each needed a tailored view — but of the same underlying claim data, not forks.

04 — UX Thinking

Collapse eleven steps into five — across three screens.

Smart defaults removed re-entry. Progressive disclosure clustered related decisions. What remained was a five-step flow across three screens — faster, but with no loss of the rigor the process required.

Role-based views on a shared component library.

High-density data tables, semantic status indicators, and role-based views — agents, handlers, and admins each saw a tailored interface composed from one shared library, scaled across all four product lines.
